against the deployment. The harness lives outside `src/` and adds no dependency to the application; the method
and its limitations are documented in [`loadtest/README.md`](../loadtest/README.md) and summarized below.

## What the numbers say

**The cache earns its place — 6.4× at p95.** An uncached search costs 29.69 ms at p95, the same search served
from Redis costs 4.65 ms (4.2× on the average). This was measured deliberately rather than by accident: the
cold scenarios vary `minPrice` per request so every one of them misses, which was confirmed against Redis —
50 varied requests produced 0 keyspace hits, 50 misses and 50 stored keys, while 50 identical requests
produced 49 hits, 1 miss and 1 key.

**Faceting is the most expensive path, and it is affordable.** `facets_cold` applies four filters and computes
every facet in a `filter` sub-aggregation that re-applies all the *other* selections (design D4) — the
highest-risk logic in the service. It costs 12% more than a plain search at p95 (33.20 vs 29.69 ms) and 16%
more on average. The design that lets a user widen a dimension they already narrowed is not paid for in
latency.

**Type-ahead has the headroom it needs.** Autocomplete answers at 4.67 ms p95, far inside the ~100 ms budget a
type-ahead box needs to feel instantaneous, and it stays there while the whole battery hammers the same stack.

**Browse mode is nearly free.** With no `q` there is no scoring to do: 5.00 ms p95 against 29.69 ms for a
scored query, which is the expected shape for `match_all` plus a sort.

**Nothing bent up to 50 concurrent users.** The ramp reached 50 VUs with p95 at 20.50 ms and not one failed
request; the saturation point is above the range tested on this hardware. Two independent runs of the whole
battery agreed within roughly 20% per scenario, so these are not one-off numbers.

**Zero failures in 366,306 requests.** No 5xx, no timeouts, no malformed payloads across 1.46 million response
assertions — the error handling and the connection lifecycle held under sustained load.

**Production latency is hosting, not code.** 297 ms average against 13.50 ms locally: the gap is the internet
round-trip to Ohio plus a free shared instance in front of managed Elastic Cloud and Upstash, not the
application. The deployment answered every request correctly with a fully valid contract.

## Honest limits of this measurement

- **The corpus is 24 documents.** Retrieval is therefore nearly free, and what the battery really measures is
  the *service's own overhead* — HTTP pipeline, validation, query assembly, aggregation handling,
  serialization. On a production-sized index Elasticsearch's contribution grows; the shape of this code does
  not change, but these absolute numbers would.
- **Load generator and service share a machine**, so they compete for CPU. Comparisons between scenarios are
  sound; absolute throughput would be higher with a dedicated generator.
- **`constant-vus` is a closed-loop model**: it measures latency at fixed concurrency and does not simulate an
  arrival rate that keeps arriving as the service slows. The production smoke uses `constant-arrival-rate`.
- **The production figure is a 62-request sample** at a deliberately low rate, sized to avoid spending metered
  quota. It establishes correctness and user-visible latency, not capacity.
